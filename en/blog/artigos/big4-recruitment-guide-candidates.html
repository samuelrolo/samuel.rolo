Big4 Recruitment:
Complete Guide for Candidates

How to join Deloitte, PwC, EY or KPMG. The 5 process stages, what they assess at each round, mistakes to avoid and preparation checklists.

Samuel Rolo 19 Mar 2026 11 min read Structured & Practical

Index

  1. The 5 Recruitment Stages
  2. The Case Interview in Detail
  3. Fatal Mistakes That Eliminate Good Candidates
  4. Preparation Checklists by Stage

Getting into a Big4 is a structured, multi-stage process designed to test not just your technical skills, but your personality, resilience, and problem-solving abilities. This guide breaks down each of the 5 stages, what recruiters are looking for, and how to prepare to maximise your chances.

The 5 recruitment stages at Big4 firms, from application to final decision.

01 — The Process

The 5 Recruitment Stages

Phase 1  ·  Weeks 1–2

CV & Cover Letter — The First Filter

Automated ATS before any human eye

Big4 firms use ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems) before any human sees your document. If you don't pass this stage, you'll never get to the interview.

What the recruiter looks for in your CV:

  • Concrete and measurable results, not "I worked on projects," but "I reduced costs by 15%"
  • Proven leadership: associations, club positions, academic projects with impact
  • Relevant internships: consulting, banking, industry, even if short
  • Consistency between your background and your motivation for consulting
  • Top-tier universities and high academic average (especially for entry-level)
Case Study 01 — The CV that passed the automated screening

Sofia applied to Deloitte with a 2-page CV, full of responsibilities in passive language ("participated in," "collaborated with"). The ATS classified it as low relevance and it never reached a human recruiter.

On her second attempt, she reformulated her CV to 1 page with bullet points in the STAR format: "Led a process optimization project that reduced customer response time by 30%." She got to the motivation interview the following week.

  • Use the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result) in each bullet point
  • Keep your CV to 1 page if you have less than 3 years of experience
  • Quantify everything: "led a team" vs. "led a team of 8 people for 6 months"
  • Personalize the cover letter for each firm — copy/paste is detected
  • Mention specific projects of the firm that interest you — it shows you've done your research
Phase 2  ·  Weeks 3–4

Online Tests and Gamification

Eliminatory — no second chance

Each firm has its preferred platform and assesses different things. Do not underestimate this stage.

Deloitte
Pymetrics / Numerical Reasoning

Decision-making, cognitive patterns, memory

PwC
Arctic Shores / Verbal & Numerical

Logical reasoning, speed and accuracy

EY
Strengths Assessment / SHL

Behaviour in professional situations

KPMG
KPMG Game (Emotional AI)

Decision under pressure, empathy, collaboration

Culture, focus, and testing platform of each of the Big4 firms.

Phase 3  ·  Weeks 5–6

Motivation Interview / HR Screen

30–45 minutes · Seems informal, but it's not

This interview is conducted by a recruiter or HR Business Partner. It's a structured screening where they eliminate candidates who can't explain why they want to be there.

The most common questions:

  • "Why do you want to work in consulting?" — the most important one, prepare it thoroughly
  • "Why Deloitte/PwC/EY/KPMG and not the others?" — shows you've done specific research
  • "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?" — align with the firm's typical career progression
  • "Tell me about a time you solved a complex problem"
  • "What was your biggest failure and what did you learn from it?"
Case Study 02 — The answer that eliminated a candidate with a 17/20 GPA

Miguel had an impeccable CV: engineering, 17/20 GPA, two internships. In the EY HR screen, when asked "Why consulting?", he replied: "Because it has a competitive salary and fast progression."

He didn't make it to the next stage. The recruiter wanted to hear evidence of intellectual curiosity, a taste for solving complex problems, and a motivation that connected the candidate's personal story to the role, not a transactional reason.

  • Focus on intrinsic motivations: curiosity, impact, accelerated learning
  • Connect your answer to a concrete experience you've had — don't use abstractions
  • Avoid mentioning salary, fast progression, or prestige as your main motivation
  • Always end by connecting to the specific firm — why that one and not another
Phase 4  ·  Weeks 7–9

The Case Interview — The Most Differentiating Stage

Real business problem · Out-loud problem solving · No right answers

You will receive a real or fictional business problem and you have to structure it, analyze it, and present recommendations out loud, in real time. It's not about knowing the answer, it's about how you think.

Phase 5  ·  Weeks 10–12

Interview with Partner or Senior Manager

Assessment of maturity, leadership, and cultural fit

This is no longer about business cases, it's about you as a person. The partner wants to know if they would put you in front of a client's CFO.

02 — The Case Interview

The Case Interview in Detail

Case types by frequency, and which firms apply them most regularly.

The Winning Structure

  • 1. Clarify the problem: ask 2 to 3 questions before you start solving
  • 2. Announce your framework: "I will approach this through three dimensions..."
  • 3. Think out loud — silence is your enemy at this stage
  • 4. Use a MECE structure: Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive
  • 5. Quantify whenever possible — get to numbers, even if estimated
  • 6. Conclude with a clear and direct recommendation, without hesitation
Case Study 03 — Example of a solved case

Prompt: "Your client is a fashion retailer in Portugal. Profit has dropped by 20% in the last year. What happened and what do you recommend?"

Approach: Start by clarifying, "Is the drop in gross margin or net profit? Is it across all categories or specific?" Structure into two dimensions: Revenue (sales drop, product mix, pricing) and Cost (COGS, SG&A). Prioritize by impact, come up with hypotheses with estimated numbers, and end with a clear recommendation: "I recommend focusing first on X because it represents 60% of the estimated impact."

Most common case interview mistakes:

✗ Starting to solve before clarifying the problem
✗ Staying silent for more than 30 seconds — think out loud
✗ Using a generic framework without adapting it to the specific case
✗ Avoiding the numbers — many candidates shy away from maths out of insecurity
✗ Not reaching a recommendation — the interviewer wants a decision, not an "it depends"

03 — Fatal Mistakes

Mistakes That Eliminate Good Candidates

  • 1

    Applying to all firms with the same material

    Each Big4 has a different culture and priorities. Deloitte values innovation and technology; KPMG is more conservative and process-focused; PwC has a strong client culture; EY stands out in people development. A generic application is immediately detected.

  • 2

    Not practicing cases until you're comfortable

    Most candidates practice 3 or 4 cases. Those who get in have practiced 20 to 40. The difference is fluency — you seem natural structuring problems, you don't seem to be following a script.

  • 3

    Underestimating the motivation interview

    Many candidates spend weeks preparing for cases and 20 minutes thinking about why they want to do consulting. The recruiter can distinguish a prepared answer from a genuine one, and values the latter.

  • 4

    Not asking anything at the end

    "Do you have any questions for me?" is an opportunity, not a formality. Not having questions communicates a lack of curiosity and interest. Always prepare 2 to 3 specific and intelligent questions.

  • 5

    Giving up after a 'no'

    Many senior consultants were rejected on their first attempt. Big4 firms have re-application policies — you can usually try again after 6 to 12 months. Ask for feedback, work on the gaps, and come back stronger.

04 — Checklists

Preparation Checklists by Stage

CV & Application

  • Your CV is at most 1 page long (if you have less than 3 years of experience)
  • Each bullet point has a concrete and measurable result
  • The cover letter mentions specific projects or initiatives of the firm
  • You have aligned your background with the area you are applying for (management consulting, technology, audit, tax)
  • You have asked someone from outside your field to review your CV — clarity counts

Online Tests

  • You have done at least 3 practice tests for numerical and verbal reasoning
  • You have a quiet environment and a stable internet connection for the test day
  • You have checked the deadline for taking the test — don't leave it to the last minute
  • You have read the full instructions before starting

Motivation Interview

  • You can explain in 2 minutes why you want to do consulting, with a concrete experience
  • You know 3 relevant differences between the firm you are applying to and its competitors
  • You have prepared behavioral answers (leadership, conflict, failure) with the STAR structure
  • You have 2 to 3 intelligent questions to ask the recruiter

Case Interview

  • You have practiced at least 15 to 20 complete cases — out loud, not just in your head
  • You have done mock interviews with other candidates or friends
  • You know how to calculate percentages and make market estimations without a calculator
  • You are comfortable with the main frameworks: profitability, market entry, M&A
  • You know how to ask for time to think without seeming blocked

Final Interview with Partner

  • You have researched the partner on LinkedIn and know their area of expertise
  • You have 3 leadership stories prepared in STAR format
  • You have prepared 3 specific questions about the firm or area — not about salary
  • You have chosen professional attire appropriate for the firm's context
  • You have confirmed the address, floor, and interviewer's name in advance

A final note: Getting into a Big4 is difficult. But it's a difficulty that can be prepared for. Most candidates who don't get in didn't fail for lack of talent, they failed for lack of specific preparation. Know what you're going to find, practice what you're going to do, and present yourself with genuine confidence. That's what separates those who get in from those who watch.
