The Future of Big4 Consulting:
How to position yourself for the next 5 years

Consulting is changing faster than firms publicly admit. AI, market consolidation, new delivery models and more demanding clients are redefining what it means to be a valuable consultant. Those who understand this today have a five-year head start.

There is a conversation happening inside the Big4 that rarely makes it into recruitment presentations or institutional LinkedIn posts. It is the conversation about what is going to happen to half of the junior and mid-level consulting roles over the next five years. The answer is not yet consensus within the firms. But the data that exists is clear enough for any professional in the sector to make informed decisions about their positioning.

This article is not about alarmism. It is about analytical clarity applied to your career.

The risk is not AI replacing consultants. It is consultants who know how to use AI replacing those who do not.

01 — The Market

What is changing in the consulting market

The global management consulting market is worth approximately $380 billion in 2025 and continues to grow. But the internal structure of that growth has changed significantly over the last three years. Three dynamics are structurally redrawing the sector.

Trend 01

The commoditisation of standard services is accelerating

Market analyses, benchmarking, process diagnostics and due diligence reports that previously required teams of 4-6 consultants over weeks are being compressed. Generative AI tools can produce in hours what used to take days. Clients have understood this and are pressing prices down on lower-complexity projects. Firms that still depend heavily on this type of work are seeing their margins squeezed.

Trend 02

Clients have developed stronger internal capabilities

The pandemic accelerated the internalisation of capabilities that were previously typically outsourced. Mid-size and large companies have built internal strategy, analytics and digital transformation teams. What these clients buy from the Big4 today is not basic execution capacity. It is external perspective, access to industry benchmarks and risk management on high-complexity projects. The consultant who only executes has lost relevance.

Trend 03

Vertical specialisation is replacing horizontal generalisation

For decades, the consulting model valued the brilliant generalist: someone who could solve any problem in any sector. That model has not disappeared, but it is being complemented by a growing demand for deep sectoral and technical specialisation. The highest-value projects with the strongest margins are concentrated today in areas such as applied AI, energy transition, financial regulation and supply chain transformation. And they require profiles that combine technical expertise with organisational navigation capability.

The value pyramid has inverted: what was once at the top has moved to the base — and what was at the base has moved to the top

02 — AI

How AI is redefining the consultant's work

The conversation about AI in consulting has two symmetric errors: those who think it will change everything overnight, and those who think it will change almost nothing. The reality is more granular — and more actionable.

The impact of AI is not uniform. It varies significantly depending on the type of work. The table below organises typical consultant activities by the expected level of AI impact over the next three to five years.

Activity AI Impact What changes
Research and information synthesis Very high From days to hours; junior research devalues significantly
Data analysis and financial modelling High Automation of standard models; premium on interpretive analysis
Report and presentation production High Structure and first draft generated by AI; human curation and narrative
Operational process diagnostics Medium AI accelerates pattern identification; contextual interpretation remains human
Stakeholder management and client dynamics Low Remains intensively human; AI supports preparation
Strategic decision-making in ambiguous contexts Low AI provides scenarios; the final judgement is human and organisational
Relationship and business development Low Trust and long-term relationships are irreducibly human

The pattern is clear: AI strongly compresses execution work and standard content production, and barely touches relationship work, judgement and leadership in high-ambiguity contexts. For a consultant at 3-7 years of career, this means the time spent on research, standard modelling and presentation formatting will decrease. What will be expected in return is more higher-level thinking, more client management and more ability to turn AI outputs into decision-relevant insight.

The practical implication: if your current differentiator is "I do analysis very well" or "I produce excellent presentations", you need to build the next differentiator urgently. If your differentiator is "I know how to navigate complex organisations and turn insights into decisions", you are well positioned for the next five years.

03 — The Profiles

The profiles that will grow — and those that will disappear

Based on market trends and structural changes at the Big4, it is possible to identify four profile archetypes and their likely trajectory over the next five years.

Positioning matrix — the highest-growth quadrant is the Hybrid Expert, combining technical specialisation with strong relationship and leadership capability

Commodity Executor: the profile with greatest risk over the next five years. Performs standard execution work well, but lacks strong technical or relational differentiation. This is the profile most exposed to automation and price compression on standard projects.

Technical Specialist: growing but with a ceiling. Masters a specific technical area (AI, ESG, financial regulation, supply chain) but has weaker ability to navigate complex organisational dynamics. Has a market, but is replaceable by another technical specialist.

Trusted Advisor: profile with a stable and growing market, particularly at senior levels. Builds long-term trust relationships with the C-suite and knows how to transform complexity into decisions. The risk is dependence on personal relationships without sufficient technical substance for the highest-complexity projects.

Hybrid Expert: the profile of greatest demand and greatest scarcity. Combines deep technical specialisation with strong leadership and relationship management capability. This is who clients want for the highest-value projects and who firms want for leadership positions. It is also the hardest profile to build and the one that takes the longest to develop deliberately.

04 — Framework

Framework: how to assess your position today

Before defining actions, it is necessary to understand where you stand. The following framework structures the assessment across four dimensions. For each one, the question is not "am I good at this?" — it is "am I good at this compared to what the market will require in five years?"

Dimension 01

Deep technical specialisation

Do you have an area of specialisation where your knowledge is significantly above market average? Not a vague sector (e.g. "finance") but a specific intersection (e.g. "banking regulation in the European Union", "operations transformation with generative AI", "energy transition strategy for industrials"). The more specific and harder to replicate, the more valuable.

Dimension 02

AI amplification capability

Do you know how to use AI tools to multiply your analytical and production capacity? It is not about knowing every model that exists, but about having enough practical fluency to do in hours what a peer takes days to do. This dimension is rapidly becoming a baseline differentiator — within two years, those without this fluency will be at a structural disadvantage.

Dimension 03

Network and reputation outside the firm

Does your professional reputation exist outside the walls of the firm where you work? Do you have a LinkedIn presence with your own opinions on your sector? Have you spoken at conferences? Have you published content that generated recognition? External reputation is an asset that protects your market position regardless of what happens internally at the firm.

Dimension 04

Business generation capability

Can you identify business opportunities and contribute to the firm's commercial development, or is your role exclusively delivery? This dimension becomes critical from Senior Manager onwards. Firms facing margin pressure are reorganising around those who bring in business, not just those who deliver projects.

The practical exercise: rate yourself from 1 to 5 on each dimension and identify your weakest dimension. That is where you should concentrate investment over the next 12 months. Improving what is already strong has diminishing returns; eliminating a structural weakness has accelerating returns.

05 — The Actions

The 5 actions that define the next 5 years

Positioning is not built with a single decision. It is built through the accumulation of small deliberate actions over time. These five actions have the greatest impact on the position of a mid-to-senior consultant over a five-year horizon.

Action 01

Define your specialisation intersection in the next 6 months

Identify the intersection between the sector or technical area where you have worked most, the market trends with the greatest growth over the next five years, and what genuinely fascinates you intellectually. The highest-value consultant in 2030 is not the best generalist — it is whoever masters a specific intersection that the market values and that is hard to replicate quickly.

Action 02

Invest 2 hours per week in AI fluency

It is not about completing a course and forgetting it. It is about integrating AI tools into your real workflow, deliberately and progressively. Start with your highest-volume activity: research and synthesis, data analysis, presentation preparation. Define a concrete goal: in 90 days, which activity can you do in half the time with AI support?

Action 03

Publish one substantive opinion per month on LinkedIn

Not generic content about leadership or soft skills. A specific opinion on your area of specialisation, with data, with a clear position and with practical implications. Twelve posts per year for three years builds a presence the market recognises. Most senior consultants do not do this. Those who do accumulate a reputational advantage that cannot be bought and cannot be quickly replicated.

Action 04

Build 3 client relationships beyond the current project

Each quarter, identify three people on the client side with whom you have a relationship but no active project. Schedule a 30-minute conversation with no commercial agenda, genuinely interested in what is happening in their business. These conversations have two returns: early identification of opportunities, and consolidation of your position as Trusted Advisor before any project emerges.

Action 05

Assess your career position in a structured way once a year

Not the firm's formal review. Your own assessment, with your own criteria: where do I stand on the four framework dimensions? What does the external market offer me today? What has changed in my sector over the last 12 months? What do I need to build over the next 12 months to be well positioned in five years? This discipline of structured reflection is rare. Those who have it navigate transitions at much lower cost.

The best career decision of the next 5 years is not where you work. It is what you build that only you can build.
